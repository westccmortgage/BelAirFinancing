How to Get Pre-Approved for a Mortgage

What it is, what it takes, and why it strengthens your offer.

In a competitive market, a well-prepared buyer stands out. Understanding pre-approval — and how it differs from a quick pre-qualification — is one of the most useful steps you can take before you start touring homes.

Pre-qualification vs. pre-approval

A pre-qualification is a quick, informal estimate based on information you share. A pre-approval is more thorough: it involves a documented review of your income, assets, and credit, resulting in a letter that carries more weight with sellers.

Neither is a commitment to lend, but a pre-approval signals that you have done the work.

Why it matters

In markets like Bel Air and the Westside, sellers and their agents take pre-approved buyers more seriously. It shows you are serious and prepared, and it clarifies your realistic range before you fall for a home outside it.

It can also surface anything worth addressing early — while there is still time to address it.

What you will typically provide

Expect to share identification, income documentation (such as W-2s and tax returns, or bank statements and entity returns if you are self-employed), recent asset statements, and authorization for a credit review.

The exact list depends on how your income and assets are structured.

For complex or high-value profiles

Self-employed borrowers, those qualifying on assets, and jumbo buyers benefit most from preparing a complete picture up front. Presenting entity income, reserves, and liquidity clearly is often the difference between a smooth process and a stalled one.

This is exactly where a financing strategy review helps — organizing the story before it reaches an underwriter.

Pre-approval is based on the information reviewed at the time and remains subject to underwriting, property review, appraisal, title, insurance, and program availability. It is not a commitment to lend, nor a guarantee of any particular rate, term, or approval.
